The Man in the Bowler Hat  Charlie Chaplin endured and, amazingly, survived the kind of childhood that makes the young Charles Dickens’s stint at the blacking factory seem like a day at Disneyland. If his youth taught Chaplin anything, it was that life was a vicious struggle against the forces of scarcity where the weak go to the wall: You had to be faster, cleverer, better than anyone else. This helps explain both the vulgarity and the ferocity of Chaplin’s earliest films. The alleviation of hunger, with which Chaplin was well acquainted, would always be a leitmotif in his work.
